  3. Elsewhere, I have been accused of being a cheerleader for H.264. I assure you, that is not the case. It is with great sadness that I acknowledge that, at least in the short term, H.264 is the de facto standard for video on the web, despite being patent-encumbered and actively licensed. Some people claim that it’s “not too late” to establish a royalty-free standard for HTML5 video, but I think that time has largely come and gone. The time to fight for that was years ago, when Apple started pushing H.264 as the default codec in QuickTime 7, then Adobe added H.264 support in Flash 9.0.something-or-other. HTML5 video, like all successful features on the web, is incremental and evolutionary. The fact of the matter is that there is a huge base of legacy already-encoded video on the web, and it’s not in a royalty-free format because — up until very recently — there was no way to deliver royalty-free video to web users. There’s still no way to do so, if you’re honest with yourself and count IE users (dominant in desktop browsing) and iPhone users (dominant in mobile browsing — not mobile in general, but certainly mobile web browsing). Those facts need to change first, before we can start realistically talking about replacing H.264. Anyone who says that it’s “not too late” is just deluding themselves. It’s already several years too late.

    — Mark

When we got there, I started poking around online for tips and tricks on how to travelling light, and came across onebag.com. I ordered this bag and it was waiting on the doorstep when I got home. I donated my entire luggage set (which was pretty ragged anyway) and I’ve been using this one bag exclusively ever since.

    I frequently travel for 5-6 days at a time for business (either to Google campus in Mountain View, or to speak at tech conferences), and I take… one bag. That includes clothes, cords, cables, and a full-size laptop. I went on a back-to-back trip to Prague and then to Moscow for GDD last fall, and I took… one bag. I’ve traveled the hell out of this bag, and it still looks and feels brand new.

    Admittedly, $250 sounds a lot of money for one bag. My wife objected when I first bought it — until she saw it. As the old saying goes (paraphrased), “If you think good luggage is expensive, try bad luggage.” My point is, this is the highest quality luggage I’ve ever had, and for the quality, $250 is not a lot of dough.
