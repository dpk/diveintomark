Vote early and often

(blogging, docbook, gfdl, gpl, python)

Dave Winer: Scripting News Awards for 2001. Don’t just sit there; vote for me!

Modesty prevents me from voting for myself. It does not, however, prevent me from writing a Python script to, um, assist you in voting for me. (Hey, the nomination is for Best Scripting Weblog.) However, fatigue prevents me from doing so at the moment. And don’t worry, Dave, honor would prevent me from publishing such a script if I did end up writing it. (That thar’s a whole lotta preventin’ goin’ on.) But you gotta think that someone is going to figure it out. People cheat at everything. Hell, people were cheating to get into the high scores of the prime number shitting bear. Vote early and often.

One interesting note: I was almost up against Eric Soroos in the Best Scripting Weblog category. That’s just too freaky for words. I knew Eric years ago, in another life. We did not part on the best of terms. Three days before I got fired, he emailed me saying that he had read this weblog and wondered how I was doing. I procrastinated responding, not quite knowing what to say after 10 years. Three days later, his was one of the messages sitting in my inbox on my company-assigned laptop when it got hastily backed up, burned to CD, stuffed in a drawer, and never looked at again so I could return the laptop to its rightful owner and get down to the business of being unemployeed.

I wonder if Eric is a member of my karass.

Slashdot: Free & Non-Free Documentation. I am please to announce that I am now starting the GNU Writing Movement with help from the GNU project. We will provide links to existing free documents, with a possibility to rate the documentation quality.

Many people don’t know that the Free Software Foundation, which brought the world the wonderfully controversial GNU General Public License for software, has also written the GNU Free Documentation License for technical writing. Well, theoretically you could use it for any kind of writing, but its purpose was to allow for Free documentation for Free software. I used the FDL for “Dive Into Python”. It is also the license of How to think like a computer scientist (Python edition), DocBook: The Definitive Guide, and many others.

The GNU Free Documentation License is viral in the same sense that the GPL is viral; derivative works must remain under the FDL. This applies to printed versions; a publisher can sell hardcopies, but they must make the same content available online for free. It also applies to translations; for example, “Dive Into Python” has 4 published translations, 2 soon-to-be-published, and 3 in progress or getting started.
